The Balance Sheet Equation Module 1

Fundamental Identity
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
Expanded
Assets = Liabilities + Share Capital + Retained Earnings

How Retained Earnings Change

  • + Revenue — increases retained earnings
  • − Expenses — decreases retained earnings
  • − Dividends — decreases retained earnings
Every transaction must keep the equation in balance. The transaction worksheet approach (each row = one transaction affecting at least two columns) is the backbone of Modules 1–3.

Capitalize vs Expense IAS 16 IAS 38

Capitalize

Cost provides future economic benefit beyond the current period. Goes on the Balance Sheet as an asset. Spread via depreciation/amortization.

Expense

Cost is consumed in the current period. Hits the Income Statement immediately. Reduces current-period profit.

Financial Statement Impact (Year 1)

If you...AssetsEquityNet IncomeOperating CF
CapitalizeHigherHigherHigherHigher
ExpenseLowerLowerLowerLower
Exam trap: Total expense over the asset's full life is identical. Capitalizing only shifts when the cost hits profit — it's a timing difference, not a permanent one.

The Matching Principle

Expenses are recognised in the same period as the revenue they help generate. This drives:

  • Capitalisation vs expensing decisions
  • Depreciation and amortisation
  • Accruals and prepayments
  • Warranty provisioning at time of sale (not at time of claim)

Revenue Recognition IFRS 15

The 5-Step Model

  • Step 1: Identify the contract with the customer
  • Step 2: Identify performance obligations (distinct goods/services)
  • Step 3: Determine the transaction price (what you expect to receive)
  • Step 4: Allocate the price to each performance obligation
  • Step 5: Recognise revenue when/as each obligation is satisfied
Revenue is recognised when control transfers to the customer, not necessarily when cash is received. Subscription revenue is recognised ratably over the period.

Deferred Revenue (Contract Liability)

Cash received before the performance obligation is satisfied → recorded as a liability.

T-Account
Opening + Cash Received − Revenue Recognised = Closing
Rearranged to find cash
Cash Received = Closing − Opening + Revenue Recognised

Journal Entries (Subscription Example)

Sale of Subscription
DR  Cash
CR  Deferred Subscription Income (liability)
Monthly Delivery
DR  Deferred Subscription Income
CR  Revenue
Current ratio impact: Deferred revenue is a current liability. Removing it (by recognising revenue) increases the current ratio.

Government Grants IAS 20

  • Gross presentation: Show subsidy as a separate income line
  • Net presentation: Deduct subsidy from the related expense
  • Both methods are acceptable under IAS 20 — must disclose the method chosen
Exam context (Eat Out to Help Out): Government pays £5 of a £10 meal. Revenue = full £10 (customer's £5 + government's £5). The subsidy is part of the consideration for the performance obligation.

Accounts Receivable & Bad Debts

Net Receivables
Gross Receivables − Allowance for Doubtful Accounts
  • Bad debt expense reduces P&L; increases the allowance
  • Writing off a specific receivable reduces both gross AR and the allowance — no P&L impact

Depreciation Methods IAS 16

Straight-Line (most common)
(Cost − Salvage Value) / Useful Life = Annual Depreciation
Sum-of-Years-Digits (Year k of n)
[(n − k + 1) / (n × (n+1) / 2)] × (Cost − Salvage)
Double Declining Balance
2 × (1 / Useful Life) × Book Value
Partial-year depreciation: Always check for pro-rata. Asset purchased 1 Jan, sold 1 Oct = 9/12 of annual depreciation in the final year.
Land is NOT depreciated. Buildings, equipment, and vehicles are.

Disposal of Fixed Assets

Net Book Value at Sale
NBV = Cost − Accumulated Depreciation (at sale date)
Gain or Loss on Disposal
Gain / Loss = Sale Proceeds − NBV
Proceeds > NBV

Gain → Credit to income statement

Proceeds < NBV

Loss → Charge to income statement

Fixed Asset Reconciliation (T-Account)

Given opening/closing balances and some figures, solve for unknowns:

Cost T-Account
Opening + Additions − Disposals (at cost) = Closing
Accumulated Depreciation T-Account
Opening + Dep Charge − Disposals (accum dep) = Closing
This is a frequently tested pattern (Practice Exam Q3–Q5, Diageo Q4 & Q6). Plug known values and solve for the unknown.

Prepaid Expenses

Advance payments. The expense is recognised in the period it benefits, not when cash is paid.

Insurance Example (Exam Q6)
Policy: £120,000 for 24 months (Oct 2023 – Sep 2025)
Monthly: £120,000 / 24 = £5,000/month
FY 2024 = Jan–Dec = 12 months
Expense: 12 × £5,000 = £60,000
Using Up Prepaid
DR  Expense (Rent, Insurance, etc.)
CR  Prepaid Asset

Intangible Assets & Goodwill IAS 38

  • Identifiable intangibles (patents, trademarks, customer lists): recognised at fair value in acquisition
  • Goodwill = excess of purchase price over fair value of identifiable net assets acquired
  • Goodwill is NOT amortised (both IFRS and US GAAP post-2001)
  • Goodwill IS tested annually for impairment

R&D Accounting IAS 38 US GAAP

US GAAP

All R&D expensed as incurred. No exceptions for development costs.

IFRS (IAS 38)

Research: Always expensed.
Development: Capitalise IF all 6 criteria met.

The 6 Development Criteria (T-I-A-F-R-C)

LetterCriterion
TTechnical feasibility
IIntention to complete
AAbility to use or sell
FFuture economic benefits probable
RResources available to complete
CCost reliably measurable
All 6 must be met simultaneously. If even one fails, expense everything.

R&D Capitalisation “What-If” Exam Favourite

If R&D were capitalised and amortised over N years instead of expensed:

Asset on Balance Sheet
Sum of unamortised portions of past N years' R&D
Impact on Net Income
(R&D Expensed − Amortisation Charge) × (1 − Tax Rate)
P&G Example (3-year amortisation)
R&D: 1999=$1,726M, 1998=$1,546M, 1997=$1,469M
Capitalised asset at June 30, 1999:
1546 × (1/3) + 1726 × (2/3) = $1,666M
Net income impact:
[1726 − (1726/3 + 1546/3 + 1469/3)] × (1 − 0.35)
= $94.7M higher

Impairment IAS 36

Impairment Exists When
Carrying Amount > Recoverable Amount
Recoverable Amount
HIGHER of: Fair Value Less Costs to Sell  vs  Value in Use
Impairment Loss
Carrying Amount − Recoverable Amount

After Impairment

  • Write down asset to recoverable amount on the Balance Sheet
  • Charge the loss to the Income Statement
  • Recalculate depreciation: New Carrying Amount / Remaining Life
Copyright (Exam Q5)
Cost: £50,000 • Useful life: 10 years
Annual amort: 50,000 / 10 = £5,000
Accum (2020–2022): 5,000 × 3 = £15,000
NBV at 1 Jan 2023: 50,000 − 15,000 = £35,000
2023 amort: £5,000
Impairment: 30,000 − 0 = £30,000
Total 2023 expense: 5,000 + 30,000 = £35,000

Amortisation & Useful Life

Amortisation (Straight-Line)
(Cost − Residual) / Useful Life

Use the shorter of legal life vs economic useful life. Residual usually assumed zero for intangibles.

Average Total Life
Gross Carrying Amount / Annual Depreciation
Average Remaining Life
(Gross − Accumulated Dep) / Annual Depreciation

Diageo Q2: Use net sales (excluding excise duties), not gross revenue, when calculating cash collected from customers.

Why Depreciation Is Added Back

Depreciation reduced net income but involved no cash outflow. Adding it back reverses that non-cash deduction so OCF reflects actual cash generated. It does NOT “generate” cash.

When R&D is capitalised instead of expensed, the cash outflow is reclassified from operating to investing — so OCF appears higher.

Bond Accounting (Effective Interest Method) Module 5

ScenarioCoupon vs MarketLiability vs Face
At parCoupon = MarketLiability = Face
At discountCoupon < MarketLiability < Face
At premiumCoupon > MarketLiability > Face
Interest Expense
Beginning Carrying Value × Market Rate at Issuance
Cash Coupon Payment
Face Value × Coupon Rate
Amortisation of Discount
Interest Expense − Cash Coupon (increases carrying value)
Carrying value converges to face value at maturity.
Autonomy Bond Example
Face: £6M • Coupon: 9% • Market: 11%
Raised: £5,706,798 (discount)
Year 1 interest expense:
5,706,798 × 11% = £627,748 (≈ £628K)
Year 1 coupon payment:
6,000,000 × 9% = £540,000
Discount amortisation:
627,748 − 540,000 = £87,748

Provisions IAS 37

Three Conditions (all must be met)

  • Present obligation from a past event (legal or constructive)
  • Probable outflow of economic benefits (>50% likely)
  • Reliable estimate of the amount can be made

Provision vs Contingent Liability

ProvisionContingent Liability
ProbabilityProbable (>50%)Possible
Balance SheetRecognisedNOT recognised
Income StatementExpense bookedNo impact
DisclosureNote + B/S lineNote only
Remote = no disclosure needed. Probable = >50% (NOT “virtually certain”).

Provision Movements

  • Charged during year → new provision expense (operating)
  • Unwinding of discount → finance cost (NOT operating)
  • Utilisations → cash paid out (reduces provision, no P&L effect)
  • Released/reversed → no longer needed (credit to P&L)

Events After Reporting Period IAS 10

Adjusting Events

Conditions existed at reporting date. New info confirms what was already true.

→ Adjust the financial statements

Court case settled, customer goes bankrupt, fraud discovered

Non-Adjusting Events

Conditions arose after reporting date. Genuinely new developments.

→ Disclose in notes only

Natural disaster, major acquisition, share price decline
